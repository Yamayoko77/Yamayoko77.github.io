The Myriennium Bug

The Last Three Hours Before Leaving Town
April 27, 2026

Bow down at my feet, you children of pylons and supermarket receipts!

They’ve finally quieted down. They were a bit loud earlier, and I had to tie up several of the bin bags gathered around me, raise my trekking pole and swing it again and again before order returned. I drape a pale blue children’s bib over my body as a cloak, which works quite well.

The room is dark, for the water and electricity were cut off. Everything has been settled; what remains are things not worth taking, going about their own affairs in every corner of the room, obediently murmuring Yes, my king! in their tiny voices. Outside the window, a plane tree with sparse yellow leaves sways gently, casting the dim amber light at my feet.

These are the last three hours before I leave this town.

No one has come to see me off. I drank four pints of cider alone at the pub downstairs, then went calmly back upstairs to continue packing. In my swaying vision, my unkempt hair falls into the corner of my eyes, looking like a willow tree stripped of all its leaves.

Three hours is not long. The first three hours of farewell in my life are still vivid. It was July 2000. A new silver bridge had just emerged across the Thames; I was sitting on the long slope at the center of the newly opened museum, people streaming past me. I had my only backpack by my side. The crowd greatly diluted my panic, while the cold, grand modern building and the warm wind from Bankside made me feel full of ambition.

But this is the twentieth departure of my life. I silently look at my luggage, with little strength left.

These are the last two hours before I leave this town.

I finally move. I crouch down and stuff a few clothes into the gaps of my suitcase. The zipper broke once; I bought a new one and sewed it on myself, which looks a bit amusing. Later it split again from the other side, and I couldn’t be bothered to fix it, so I just strapped it shut. The suitcase therefore acquired the air of a prisoner. I have escorted this prisoner across three countries and twenty addresses. It has never once protested.

Then I put the MP3 player into the suitcase. A Sony Walkman, slim and silver, so-called the perfume bottle, with a few hundred Synthwave and nu-jazz tracks loaded. Back then I had saved up for a long time to buy it. Soon after in the pub I downloaded the songs, put on the headphones, the silver blood inside the perfume bottle pumping into my body. My boys thumped my back, shouting, reaching to yank the headphones off me. I covered my face with the corners of my mouth pulling up. I once had a stack of vinyls and a decent turntable too. I sold them all. I only kept this one.

Next to it, in a plastic bag, is a stack of exhibition pamphlets. Back when I was with her, we were fond of the exhibitions. I still remember the way she would pick up pamphlets in the gallery and tuck them into her little notebook. So I kept plenty too. I only pull them out when I’m packing to move, but I’ve never planned to throw them away. I still hold onto an illusion that I am still leaning into her soft chest, watching her swing her red patent heels, in her low-cut floral dress, scooping a little spoon of affogato to her mouth, murmuring to herself as she scribbles in her little notebook. Illusions are good things.

Beside that bag is another plastic bag wrapped around my ticket collection book. Once I had drifted on the swaying GWR through the fields of south-west England and Wales. I was obsessed with the railway, obsessed with buying any random ticket to any village, then wandering across the moors. As dark fell I’d go to the pub by the station and drink until I was wrecked and until the train came. Then I would board, curling into the corner of my seat, listening to music, and watching the last crimson gleam over the dim wide moor through the window. Sometimes the train would gallop through small stations with brightly lit platforms flashing past. The water tanks, the blast furnaces, and the pylons walked slowly in the distance. People would pinch their noses and keep their distance from me. That sort of thing is easy to notice.

I zip up the suitcase and tighten the drawstring on the trash bag. I put on my waxed jacket and pull on my boots. These two have been with me through plenty of places. I think back to those days walking down roads hedged with bramble and blackthorn, crossing pastures teeming with sheep and cattle, traversing woodland trails where mud and fallen leaves mingled. I walked in the rain across the moors, gazing into the distance at the power pylons. Amidst the clamour of the rain I could run and stop and run and stop and weep aloud. I would imagine myself to be an ascetic hero like Odysseus, though I had to be careful to avoid farmers and police.

The amber tree shadow in my vision is still swaying. This is the last hour before I leave this town.

The guy next door is watching TV and bursting into canned laughter. And here in this little ten-square-meter room with peeling wallpaper and warped floorboards, what’s left in front of me is: half a tube of toothpaste, a slice of pizza, a train ticket for tonight. I put them respectively into the outer pocket of the suitcase, into my mouth, and into my coat pocket. Then I pull out the little bib I’d forgotten about, tucked inside my jacket, and let it hang down my back. I sweep it backwards, pick up my hiking pole again, and face the bin bags. With my back to the window, I bend at the waist, lower my head, close my eyes, and imagine a new sovereign kneeling on one knee before me, both hands raised, waiting for me to pass him the sceptre.

The loosely tied bib slips down to my chest. I look at the stupid blue teddy bear printed on it. A highchair, a laughing woman, a blue plastic bowl held up and shaken. When was that?

Then the figure in the room starts staggering. I open my eyes and look out at the dull amber daylight beyond the window. It has started to rain.

The bugs don’t care about any of this. They run their tiny dynasties in the folds of the trash bag, neither supporting nor opposing my reign and my abdication. They just happen to be there. This is what makes them my most honest subjects. At least they would not say things like “Keep in touch!” and “I love you!” I don’t know.

I pull the suitcase and kick the trash bags out of the door and tuck the keys under the mat.

These bugs will live until the next tenant arrives. Maybe longer. Maybe they really can live ten thousand years, which would make them better suited than me to own an empire. I don’t know.

I pick up my trekking pole, let out a laugh, and announce once more to the tiny stirrings deep inside the bags: Today I set out on my expedition. Carry on as you were.

No reply. Perhaps the cider is wearing off a little.
