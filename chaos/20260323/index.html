Spring Robins

March 23, 2026

Boris was found dead in his bed. The room had been turned over: a computer swept to the floor, a power cord torn loose, books ripped to pieces. The air carried a wet earthy smell. He lay there like a malaria patient, wrapped tight in thick blankets in the late spring, face turned toward the dim grey light out of the window. From his mouth and nostrils poured flowers — daffodils, daisies, tulips, ranunculi, violets. They came in gorgeous colours, spilling from the dark, swollen gap of his open mouth, heaping over the mattress, across the floor, creeping through the window seam, forcing back the frame limiter and climbing the grey wall of the building outside.

Annie bought a blouse at a designer boutique in London. The blouse was priced at two hundred and thirty pounds: violet iridescent poplin, three-quarter sleeves, length sixty-five centimetres. Its tiered ruff collar bloomed open like petals and a fragrance trailing down from the neckline. In the mirror, the pale-faced woman adjusted her bleached platinum hair, ruby earrings swinging. She straightened her back, and arranged her mouth into a friendly smile. Then she walked to the counter, glanced at yet another bill that had pushed through on her phone, opened her purse, and said nothing. More petals burst trembling from the wallet, crept along the floor, and swallowed the woman and the counter whole.

A few weeks earlier, on the first morning of daylight-saving time, three strangers had shared a park bench in Hyde Park for a moment. From across the lake, a mute swan beat its wings and crashed into a cherry tree. Petals scattered suddenly, into Boris’s coffee cup, into Annie’s open purse, onto Winston’s phone as it rang and rang. All three sat formally dressed, held themselves at a careful distance. None of them spoke. It seemed no more than an ordinary morning.

Still the most ordinary morning: Winston sat on the train. The carriage swung faintly, as though someone were pushing at his shoulder over and over in his sleep. It was still dark when he dragged his suitcase towards Paddington, and in his exhaustion he kept replaying his escape that morning. The cherry trees along the road, the empty streets, the pale glow of the screen. A work call at midnight, a night of turning over, a heart in his mind slowly draining of its bright crimson; earlier he had thrown away a knife that had nearly swung, and sworn to flee.

Through the carriage window he watched horses galloping in the opposite direction, toward the city. Behind them rolled a surge of wild grass, yellow daffodils, iris and churned like dust. Small stations flashed past, their platforms swathed in green, timetables and electronic boards trailing flowering vines.

The wild grass was undeniably invading. He thought of the morning’s news:

Cross Country services suspended — widespread disruption /

Jet2 launches spring holiday package, weekend return to Crete from £50 /

Holborn High Street Pret closed until further notice due to unprecedented foliage /

Brighton station smothered in flowers.

He watched his lit phone through the glass. The endless stream of notifications. Buds tapping at the window. He found his own reflection suddenly repulsive. What was this man doing here?

He looked around. The carriage had never felt so unbearably hot. It made him want to retch. The train clattered against the rails — station announcements, coughing, small child shrieking, the crisp tear of plastic packaging. As it slowed into an unnamed station, he stumbled out before the doors had fully opened.

The platform was deserted. Wind came running over the moor, carrying the bitter smell of bruised grass. He stumbled through a low fence and set off across the open ground beyond the tracks, walking faster and faster, then striding into a run. Something scalding was lodged in his throat and he could neither swallow it nor bring it up. A horse seemed to gallop pass him somewhere nearby, heading towards the city.

He stopped. After the train had gone, the moor fell silent but for the wind’s low moan. The gust raised by the horse lifted his hair, gathered dew little by little into his eyes, and little by little drained all the strength from deep inside him. Slowly he sank to his knees and collapsed onto the ground and retched twice. Fine rootlets moved softly along the inside of his ribcage, leafing his whole life deep within his body. Then the wind on the moor surged sharply, iris and weeds bowed down, birds lifting from the ground on the gust. A violent fit of coughing tore out of him and immediately transformed into loud shouts mingling with the sound of the wind. At the edge of the fields, clusters of flowers were emerging in succession, engulfing the railway tracks and the lone figure in the field, and marching boldly along the rails.

He thought suddenly of the horse: it would soon have passed Reading, passed Heathrow, come cantering into the clamorous early morning of outer London. Carrying with it the smell of soil, of wildflowers, of furious breath, it would finally go trampling over every inch of road that tyres had ever pressed.
